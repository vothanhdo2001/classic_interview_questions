# FULL SPEECH - Ace Your Interview: Mastering Classic Questions
## (~6–7 min | target ~900 words) | Speaking Challenge Format

---

**"Today, I'm going to talk about something many students forget to prepare for - job interviews."**

**"I'll start by asking you this:"**
Have you ever walked into an interview already knowing the questions - but still blanked when they actually asked them?

I think most of us have been there. And it's not because you're not good enough. It's because you weren't ready for questions you already knew were coming.

**"And then I'm going to move on to discuss 6 questions that come up in almost every interview"** - and how to answer each one in a way that actually works.

**"And finally, I'll share one idea that changes the way you see interviews."**

---

**"One thing I learned and found useful is this:"**
Interviews aren't about being perfect. They're about being prepared. Most people lose not because they're not good enough - but because they didn't practice answers to questions they already knew were coming.

So let's go through them.

---

The first one - and everyone gets this - is *"Tell me about yourself."*

This is your opening. But here's the mistake most people make: they tell their whole life story. Don't do that. Keep it to three things: your background, your main skill, and why this job fits.

Something like: *"I studied IT, I'm good at solving problems, and I want to work here because your projects are exactly what I want to be doing."*

And when you mention your experience, use phrases like *"I'm particularly proud of..."* or *"A good example that comes to mind..."* to bring in a real story. For example: *"I'm particularly proud of my timekeeping - I always plan ahead to meet deadlines."*

Short. Clear. You're already in control.

---

Now the second one: *"Why do you want this job?"*

And look - this is where most people lose points. They say *"I want to grow"* or *"I want a challenge."* But that's what everyone says.

What actually works is doing your research first. Find one real thing about the company - a project, a goal - and connect it to your own story. That's what makes you stand out.

And if you're stuck for words in the moment, just be direct: *"I really, really want this job and I am willing to work very hard if I get it."* It's simple - but it shows real motivation.

---

Okay, moving on - *"What is your greatest strength?"*

Please don't say *"I'm a hard worker."* I know it feels safe, but it sounds like everyone else.

Instead, name your strength - and then use simple connectors to walk through what happened: *"My strength is staying calm when things get difficult. Firstly, when my team's deadline moved up by one week, I didn't panic. Secondly, I made a new plan and reorganized the tasks. And above all, we finished on time."*

One strength. One story. That's all you need.

---

**"The most challenging part of preparing for interviews was learning to answer the next one: 'What is your greatest weakness?'"**

And this one is tricky. A lot of people say *"I work too hard"* - thinking it sounds good. It doesn't. It just sounds dishonest.

Here's what actually works: pick a real weakness, and show that you're doing something about it.

*"I used to avoid difficult conversations. So I started asking for feedback after every project - and that really helped me improve."*

That answer is honest. And honest answers build trust.

---

**"I'm proud of myself because I learned to stop being afraid of the next one: 'Tell me about a mistake you made.'"**

Most people think this is a trap. It's not - it's actually a chance to show who you are.

Just tell the full story: what happened, what you learned, what you changed, and what got better because of it. They're not looking for perfection. They're looking for self-awareness.

---

And then there's the last question - *"Do you have any questions for us?"*

This one gets skipped all the time. People say *"No, I think we covered everything"* - and just like that, they've missed one of the best moments in the whole interview.

End with something that shows you're already thinking ahead:

*"Is there a problem your team is facing right now that you think I could help with?"*

That shows initiative. It shows you already think like part of their team.

---

**"My favorite activity in this course was the speaking practice"** - because it pushed me to say things out loud, not just think about them quietly. And that's exactly what you need in a real interview.

**"I want to keep improving my English by using it in real situations"** - job interviews, work meetings, or just real conversations outside the classroom.

**"I want to say thank you"** to our teacher and everyone in this class - because every time one of us spoke up, it made it a little easier for the rest of us to do the same. That's something I'll carry with me.

**"I'll remember this class because"** it gave me more than English skills. It gave me confidence. And in a job interview - confidence is everything.

Thank you.
